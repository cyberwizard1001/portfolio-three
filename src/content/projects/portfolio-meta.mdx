## Why this page exists

Most of the displayed work on this site points outwards, at problems faced by others. Pet insurance buyers needed reassuring, Perro users needed a quicker and more meaningful onboarding experience, mental health practitioners were lacking a single system to manage their workflows. This one is about me, as the portfolio itself has turned out to be the longest-running and most stubborn design problem so far, and I figured I might as well be honest about it.

In short, I've redesigned this site six times now and rebuilt it thrice, with each iteration having a fairly similar progression. I'll spend a couple of weeks enjoying it and designing somehting I'm proud of, and somewhere around the point where it's nearby ready, the doubts start showing up, right on schedule. Is it too much? Is it too little? Will a recruiter who spends 10 seconds on the hero section understand who I am, what I do, and what I bring to the party? Have I made the text too generic? Does it sound wishy-washy? I start slowly sanding the thing down or piling more onto it, depending on which fear got to me first that week, until I've talked myself out of liking the first draft. This is a case study about a design process that includes, as a load-bearing step, me getting in my own way.

## The pattern I keep repeating

I've learnt that it helps to name it, because once you do, you can at least know when it's happening. The cycle looks like this:

For the first two weeks I'm designing for myself, and it's great. I pick a direction that feels like me, I design it, and I like looking at it. Then the doubt creeps in and the question changes from "does this feel like me?" to "what will other people think of this?", which sounds like maturity and is actually where the trouble starts. I imagine a sceptical hiring manager, a designer friend who'll find it derivative, a developer who'll notice the corners I cut. And I start designing for that imaginary crowd instead, if that makes sense. Designing for the voices in my head.

From there, it could go one of two ways depending on how I feel. Some weeks, I convince myself it's all too noisy and that I need to strip it back to something austere and minimal, the kind of site that's mostly white space and confidence, because that's what serious people make. Other weeks, I decide it's too plain, too safe, that it doesn't show any personality or craft, and I should make it bigger and weirder and more expressive so it actually stands out and screams 'Nirmal'. I've come to realise that both of these are just fear wearing different outfits, fear of being judged for my output - not just in terms of conversion or adoption, but as a reflection of me, my skills and my values. One, is the fear of looking like I'm trying too hard, the other is the fear of being forgettable, and I've gaslit myself in both directions more times than I'd like to admit.

What I've learnt through all of this is that neither the maximalist or the minimalist insticts are wrong on their own. The mistake is letting the imaginary audience drive, because the imaginary audience doesn't actually exist and the real people who look at this site are far more generous and far less interested in catching me out than the version of them I keep inventing. The history of these redesigns is basically a record of me learning that lesson and then forgetting it again a few months later.

## V2 — the tidy correction

Then the doubt showed up at the door for the first time, and you can watch it happen between V1 and V2. Everything got quiet and orderly. Too quiet and orderly. Light background, neat rules between sections, a calm "Me." and "Works." structure, toggles for Code and Designs, a tidy little socials block at the bottom. "I design and develop clean, easy to understand digital experiences for mobile and web," which reads like someone reassuring an interviewer rather than introducing themselves.

This was the first time I traded the loud version for the safe version, and on its own merits it's a perfectly nice site. The problem is that I made it nice by sanding off most of what made V1 mine. I'd swung from "too much" all the way to "tasteful and forgettable" without stopping anywhere in the middle, which is a move I'd go on to repeat a couple times.

## V3 — the maximalist swing back

V3 is the pendulum swinging the other way, hard. Back to dark, but this time with ambition. Enormous ghosted section headings, an outlined card aesthetic, a little 3D figure with a speech bubble, a side rail of section labels, a "continuously scrolling text, random facts, could be a tech news API" idea that I clearly thought was very clever. There's a contact form, a flower illustration, the works.

This is the maximalist version of the fear, where I decided the tidy site was too forgettable and overcorrected into making something expressive enough to prove I had taste. A lot of it I still like. Some of it is me showing off. If I'm being honest to myself, V2 and V3 are the same anxiety solved two opposite ways, and you can almost date each redesign by which worry was winning that month.

## V5 — the one that actually shipped, in Flutter

V5 is the one that became a real, live, deployed site at [cyberwizard1001.github.io](https://cyberwizard1001.github.io), and it's the most resolved of the lot up to this point. We've graduated with Master's in HCI and just landed a full-time design job. "Weaving dreams into digital realities," a confident dark layout, colourful rounded project cards showing actual app work, a proper about section, a grid of skills, and a big friendly "Let's talk!" at the bottom. It pulls the minimalist discipline of V4 and the personality of the earlier ones into something that mostly holds together. This is roughly where I stopped fighting myself long enough to put a flag in the ground, and it's the first of the two sites I built in Flutter.

## portfolio_two — the design I'm still using, built the hard way

This one is special, because if you look at the current site and squint, you're basically looking at this. The second Flutter build, live at [cyberwizard1001.github.io/portfolio_two](https://cyberwizard1001.github.io/portfolio_two), is from a April 2026, and when I finally found my identity. A dark layout on a faint dotted grid, a small "Nirmal" wordmark in monospace, "Observing people, Crafting experiences" as the headline with "insight-led user experiences" sitting under it in soft purple, and the honest description, "A UX designer who builds." After five attempts I'd finally found something that felt like me and didn't make me flinch a fortnight later, which is the closest I've come to winning the argument with myself (but as you might've noticed, didn't last very long).

The work section grew up too. Instead of placeholder blocks it has proper project cards, each one carrying an overview and a few highlights, the same case studies that anchor the site now. This was the first version that had the projects being the focal point of the site rather than just being a work in progress with no real world studies.

The About section is the part I'm fondest of and the part that gives the whole thing away, harkening back to my earlier designs with the terminal. It's a fake terminal! You watch commands type themselves out, `about --nirmal`, then `skills --list`, then `contact --open` quietly opening a links panel. It's the developer half of me showing through the designer half, and it's the bit that makes this version unmistakably mine rather than a tasteful template anyone could have made.

So this was the one I was happiest with. It was also the one that finally broke my patience with the tooling, because the footer says it plainly: designed and built in Flutter. Which brings me to the part of the story I'd warn anyone else away from when they're working on portfolios.

## The Flutter web detour, and why I'd warn you off it

I want to be clear that I love Flutter. You'll see loads of Flutter projects on my Github profile, dating back to 2021. As a way to build apps it's genuinely a pleasure, and the fact that I can design something and then go and build the real thing myself is a big part of how I work and something I'd never give up. So none of what follows is me bad-mouthing the framework and is instead me being annoyed, as someone who likes it, that the web ecosystem has been lagging behind for years and shows it the moment you try to ship a website with it.

The trouble is that Flutter on the web doesn't render a normal web page. It paints onto a canvas, which means the browser doesn't really understand the thing it's showing you, and all the small behaviours you take completely for granted on a website stop being free. Selecting a line of text, the most basic thing a reader does, doesn't work by default and needs additional scaffolding. The back button, which every person alive expects to do the obvious thing, didn't reliably behave like a back button, so I ended up having to wire up routing workarounds just to make the browser act like a browser. Each of these might feel like a small niggle, but put enough small things in the way and you're spending your evenings testing minute interactions and writing new code and adding on additional maintenance baggage. I had a portfolio site which was 10k lines strong. Not ideal for a designer trying to spin up a portfolio site to showcase design work.

So after two Flutter sites and a lot of workarounds I didn't enjoy maintaining, I made the least exciting and most sensible decision of the whole saga, switching to HTML.

## The current one — plain HTML, on purpose

The version live now at [nirmalkarthikeyan.com](https://nirmalkarthikeyan.com) is, give or take, a very Linux desktop themed portfolio_two design rebuilt from scratch using Astro and good ol' HTML and CSS, lightened up and stripped of unnecessary bloat. No canvas, no fighting browsers or decades of human-internet interaction experience. Everything... just works.

Going back to basics felt almost embarrassingly freeing after years of working around a renderer. There's a lesson buried in that which I keep relearning across all of this, that the fanciest tool isn't the one that serves the work, it's the one that gets out of the way. The same is true of the design. The current site resonates with me, but this time I think it's working because it is showcasing content while still representing me and my journey into UX.

## What I'd actually defend about all this

The temptation with a page like this is to tie it up with a neat moral, draw a positive, straight-forward conclusion, but I find myself unable to do that. I don't think any of these redesigns were a mistake, even the ones I overcorrected into, because each one taught me something and led to improvements in the next version. V1 taught me I had a voice. V2 taught me how easily I'd abandon it the second I got nervous. V3 showed me where my taste actually was once I stopped being careful. V4 taught me that minimalism is a choice you make for the reader, not somewhere to hide my inabilities. V5 was me finally shipping something, and V6 was me finding the layouts I'd keep. Finally, V7, the site you're on, is me admitting that the clever framework choice was making my life harder for no benefit the visitor would ever notice, and leaning into the roots of my UX design story, the Linux desktop ricing scene and the constant scripting.

If there's anything in this journey I'd wholeheartedly defend, it's the decision to keep doing it badly in public rather than waiting until I felt sure. I have never once felt sure. Every version of this site has gone live while some part of me was still convinced it was either too much or too little. That's true for this site too, I'm sure you'll find this included as a step in my next website in a year or two. Waiting for the doubt to clear would have meant never having a site to share, because the doubt is not a bug in my process, it's the part of the process where I care. The trick is to let the doubt have its say and publish anyway, which, now that I think of it, is exactly what I'm doing by writing all of this down.
