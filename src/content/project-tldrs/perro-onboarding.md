**Problem:** Perro was a genuinely strong product, and the people who got past the first session tended to like it. That was the problem, because a large share of new users never got there. They were leaving during onboarding, often before they had seen anything that showed them what Perro could do for them, and that was costing real subscriptions.

**Approach:** Rather than redesign on instinct, I started with the Mixpanel funnels to find where people left, then watched usability sessions to understand why. The drop-offs sat in three places and the qual and the quant agreed at every one of them. The flow felt like a form rather than an introduction: optional questions treated as mandatory, a photo upload that sent people out of the app to find a better picture, and health questions arriving before anyone had a reason to care. So I shortened it, led with value through a feature carousel, swapped the mandatory photo for avatars, and added a plain-language explainer before the permission prompts.

**Constraints:** A two-week part-time sprint. It had to stay in Flutter, make no backend changes, and leave the experience stable for existing users. Being both the designer and the developer meant every idea had to be something I could actually build, which kept me honest.

**Outcome:** Drop-off fell, completion rose, and more people converted to subscriptions, with more of them choosing longer-term plans. The work was later cited as one of the product's strengths when the company was acquired. None of it worked because it looked nicer. It worked because it stopped asking people for things before it had given them a reason to say yes.
