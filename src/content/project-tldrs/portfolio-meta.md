**Problem:** Most of the work on this site points outwards, at problems other people had. This one points at me. The portfolio itself is my longest-running and most stubborn design problem, seven iterations and three full rebuilds. Every time I get most of the way there, the doubt shows up on schedule and I start arguing with myself about whether any of it is right.

**Approach:** I named the pattern, because once you do, you can at least tell when it's happening. For the first couple of weeks I design for myself and like it. Then the question quietly changes from "does this feel like me?" to "what will other people think?", and I start designing for an imaginary sceptical crowd instead. From there I either strip back to something austere or pile on more to prove I have taste, depending on which fear got to me first that week. Both are just fear wearing different outfits.

**Constraints:** My own doubt, mostly, plus the tools. Two of these sites (V5 and V6) were built in Flutter, which paints onto a canvas instead of rendering a real page, so basic web behaviours like selecting text or trusting the back button stopped being free and needed scaffolding. I ended up with a 10k-line portfolio and evenings spent wiring up workarounds, which is not where a designer's attention should go.

**Outcome:** V7, the site you're on, is V6 rebuilt from scratch in Astro and plain HTML/CSS, a Linux-desktop theme that leans into where my UX story actually started. Going back to basics felt almost embarrassingly freeing. The thing I'd defend most is doing it badly in public rather than waiting until I felt sure, because I have never once felt sure, and the doubt isn't a bug in the process. It's the part where I care.
