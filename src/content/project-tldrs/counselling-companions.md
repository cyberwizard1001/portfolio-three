**Problem:** Most counsellors and therapists work in solo or small practices, without the IT and admin support larger institutions take for granted. Their day runs across a patchwork of general-purpose tools that were never meant to work together, so a single client's information ends up scattered and they spend real time reassembling the picture. One person I interviewed put it plainly: for every 40-minute session, 15 to 20 minutes goes on admin. That time comes straight out of care.

**Approach:** My MSc dissertation moved through semi-structured interviews with seven practitioners, reflexive thematic analysis, and a week-long co-design workshop I ran entirely by post and email so it respected how little spare time they have. That fed a Figma prototype of a practice dashboard with a companion voice assistant, which I tested through heuristic evaluation, a think-aloud session, and a Responsible Research and Innovation review.

**Constraints:** A sensitive setting where trust is everything, and strong, often opposing views on AI in a therapy room. The work had to streamline the back office without touching the human relationship the whole profession rests on, and without introducing ethical risk through careless use of AI.

**Outcome:** A validated concept and prototype, with the AI deliberately kept on a short leash. It is opt-in per client rather than once for the whole practice, it explains itself, and a human reviews and approves anything before it reaches a client record. That per-client toggle and the approve-before-it-acts pattern came directly out of the ethics review, and they were the parts people responded to most strongly.
